A big part of life as a programmer is expression of creative intellect. My projects are my creative outlet, and serve as a fun and efficient wormhole to knowledge. I love picking up new skills by tackling a problem that requires an a priori grasp of the same skill.

My favorite part about some of these projects was the team bonding and friendship building that came with it. A lot of these projects would have been impossible without a high functioning team with friendship at it's very core.

CrowdPlay

A team project imagined and realized at Hack RU Spring '18, CrowdPlay has probably been my favorite work so far. Three of my best friends and I were able to create an interface for the communication of Spotify, Bose, and Twilio's APIs. Building a 'text-in' song request system, we implemented a priority queue to empower every individual at a party to be their own DJ! Songs would be queued up with respect to popularity of request so that majority wins. Functionality for querying and controlling current song, queue status, volume, etc. was also included. We were able to snag the Best Use of Twilio's API award for this creation!

Pokemon VS Digimon Classifier

As a kid I had always loved watching Pokemon. The pokemon were so unique, and unlike anything I had ever seen before. So when Digimon and it's monsters started to gain popularity, I faced a dilemma! These creatures looked identical to my beloved pokemon! I couldn't tell one from the other. Well, I guess kid me never got over this crisis, and so adult me decided to go ahead and create this.
Using fast.ai 's wonderful tutorial for their Cat VS Dog Classifier, I was able to create and clean my own dataset of meticulously chosen images of monsters from each show, and train and test this model on it. Needless to say, the machine was able to distinguish pretty much every image correctly.

Micromouse

One of my more hardware oriented undertakings, Micromouse was a very important learning experience for me. As a part of Rutgers IEEE, I worked in a team to design, 3D-print, and assemble a robot. We used and Arduino wired up to IR sensors and gyroscopes to program the bot to navigate any given maze. We used Dijkstra's algorithm to keep track of the previous visited corners in the maze. This experience enabled me to apply my classroom knowledge and actually program a machine to do my bidding. We faced multiple obstacles along the way, and struggled all through it, and even though our finished product wasn't what we expected everyone was left all the wiser.
